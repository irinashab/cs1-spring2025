Course Description

This course provides an introduction to Computer Science and programming, focusing on programming as the foundation for understanding and exploring the world of computers. Students will learn essential programming concepts and develop coding skills through hands-on practice. The course is designed to be flexible and adaptive to the needs of students, whether they are majoring in Mathematics/Computer Science or coming from other disciplines. We will begin with simple programming assignments, gradually building toward more complex tasks. By the end of the semester, students will work on individualized projects that showcase their acquired skills and align with their personal interests or academic focus.
Our primary programming language will be Processing, a Java-based language with its own Integrated Development Environment (IDE). Processing is widely recognized for its versatility and accessibility, making it an excellent choice for beginners and those interested in creative coding applications.

Course Goals

This course has two goals: to teach students to "talk to the computers in the computer's own language" and to prepare Math/CS majors for Computer Science 2 and upper-level electives.

Learning Outcomes

Upon completion of this course, successful students should be able to

  • Use basic computer terminology
  • Understand Processing syntax including
    • variables and data types
    • conditionals
    • loops
    • arrays
    • strings
  • Learn how to handle images and program simple animations
  • Make the programs interactive
  • Understand the ideas of Object-Oriented programming including
    • classses
    • methods
    • basics of inheritance
  • Do basic debugging

Homework

Homework will be assigned on each topic. Instructions will be posted on Brightspace; it’s your responsibility to check Brightspace regularly. Detailed solutions should be written up neatly, showing ALL of the work. Submitting just answers to the problems will not be counted as homework.
If you miss a class, it is your responsibility to locate and submit those assignments on time. Missing a class does not grant you an automatic extension on homework submission.

Late Homework Policy

Mathematics is extremely cumulative, so it is important to stay on top of your work when it is assigned. If you miss a class or fall behind, you still need to stay on top of the work that was covered. Do your best to stay current with the work, using all the resources at your disposal to help you.
If, due to emergencies or illnesses, you require some flexibility, you can reach out to me and we can discuss what you can do to make things up. However, if you fall behind on work and submit work late, I will have less time to actually look at it, grade it and provide meaningful feedback. If work is submitted too late, I reserve the right to simply give it a zero and move on if I do not have time to look at it.

Collaboration Policy/Academic Integrity

You are encouraged to discuss homework assignments and problem sets with other students. You must complete the assignment on your own, however. There is a clear difference between copying someone else’s work and discussing a problem with another person. The latter is encouraged. Plagiarizing another student’s work can lead to academic sanctions as per Purchase College’s Academic Integrity Policy Collaboration is not allowed on any exam, including take-home exams. We take cheating seriously!
